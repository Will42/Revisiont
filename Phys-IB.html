Topic 1: Measurements and Uncertainties

1.1 Measurements in Physics

Quantities and units

Fundamental quantities are quantities from which all other quantities are expressed in terms of. For example in the density equation $ρ = {m \over V}$ only mass is a fundamental (volume being the product of three lengths, another fundamental unit), density and volume are said to be derived quantities.

To prevent confusion on conversions of multiple scales of units, there is a worldwide standard for units called SI - Système international d'unités. The SI defines both units and prefixes - letters used to form decimal multiples or sub-multiples of the units. The units themselves are classified as being either fundamental (or base), derived, and supplementary. There are only two supplementary units: the radian (rad) – the unit of angular measurement and the steradian (sr) – the unit of “solid angle”.

Fundamental and derived units

In SI units there are only seven fundamental units. These are:

metre (m): the length of the path travelled by light in a vacuum during a time interval of $1 \over 299 792 458$ of a second.

kilogram (kg): mass equal to the mass of the international prototype of the kilogram kept at the Bureau International des Poids et Mesures at Sèveres, near Paris.

second (s): the duration of 9192631770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the caesium-133 atom

ampere (A): that constant current which, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of in nite length, negligible circular cross-section, and placed 1 m apart in vacuum, would produce between these conductors a force equal to 2 × 10–7 newtons per metre of length.

kelvin (K): the fraction $1 \over 273.16$ of the thermodynamic temperature of the tripple point of water.

mole (mol): the amount of substance of a system that contains as many elementary entities as there are atoms in 0.012 kg of carbon–12. When the mole is used, the elementary entities must be speci ed and may be atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, other particles, or speci ed groups of such particles.

candela (cd): the luminous intensity, in a given direction, of a source that emits monochromatic radiation of frequency 540 × 1012 hertz and that has a radiant intensity in that direction of $1 \over 683$ watt per steradian.

Significant Figures

Scientists use a method of rounding to a certain number of significant figures (often abbreviated to s.f.). Here "significant" means meaningful.

Rules for using significant figures:

A digit that is not a zero will always be significant – 683 is three significant figures (3 s.f.).

Zeros that occur sandwiched between non-zero digits are always significant – 5802 (4 s.f.); 19.0405 (6 s.f.).

Non-sandwiched zeros that occur to the left of a non-zero digit are not significant – 0.389 (3 s.f); 0.093 (2 s.f.).

Zeros that occur to the right of the decimal point are significant, provided that they are to the right of a non-zero digit – 1.034 (4 s.f.); 1.00 (3 s.f.); 0.34500 (5 s.f.); 0.003 (1 s.f.).

When there is no decimal point, trailing zeros are not significant (to make them significant there needs to be a decimal point) – 400 (1 s.f.); 400. (3 s.f.) – but this is rarely written.

For addition and subtraction, leave your answers in terms of the least number of decimal places. For multiplying or dividing leave your answers to the least number of significant figures from the quantities used in the question.

Scientific notation

Scientific notation is a mathematical expression used to represent a decimal number between 1 and 10 multiplied by ten, so you can write large numbers using less digits. An example of scientific notation is when you write 6.34 x 10³ for 6340.

Metric multipliers (prefixes)

There is another way of abbreviating units: by using metric multipliers (usually called “prefixes”). An SI prefix is a name or associated symbol that is written before a unit to indicate the appropriate power of 10. So instead of writing 2.5 × 1012 J we could alternatively write this as 2.5 TJ (terajoule). These prefixes can be found in the data booklet used in IB examinations.

Orders of magnitude

It is important to understand whether or not the physics or the value calculated in physics is sensible or resonable. A number rounded to the nearest 10 is known as an order of magnitude. For example, for the average mass of a human being: around 60-80 kg - powers of 102 should be used rather than 101 as it is closer to 100 kg than 10 kg.

Estimation

Estimation is used to produce a value that is a useable approximation to the true value. Estimation is related to finding orders of magnitude, but may result in a value more precise than the nearest power of 10. For example whenever you use a ruler calibrated in millimetres you can accurately measure to a millimetre but will need to estimate to the next $1 \over 10$mm.

1.2 Uncertainties and Errors

Uncertainties in measurement

No experimental quantity can be measured with 100% accuracy, there is always a degree of uncertainty. There are two types of error that contribute to this uncertainty in scentific readings: systematic and random.

Systematic errors

These errors occur due to a fault of the system. this could be due to faulty equipment or apparatus. For example digital equipment may be incorrectly calibrated due to the way it was manufactured or due to being overused. On a piece of equipment such as a mass balance the zero setting can drift such that it no longer reads zero when it should. This is called a zero error. Systematic errors can also be due to faults in the experiment and the way it is designed. When systematic errors are small, a measurement is said to be accurate.

Random errors

Random errors can occur in any measurement but occur most frequently when the experimenter has to estimate the last significant figure when reading a scale. These are likely to cause the results of the measurements to be either too big or too small. For an analogue scale, the uncertainty is ± half of the smallest scale division. For a digital scale, the uncertainty is ± 1 the least significant digit. Repeated tests help to reduce the random errors in an experiment. Readings with small random errors are said to be precise (NOT accurate).

Absolute and fractional uncertainties

Absolute uncertainties have the same units as the the quantity itself. Dividing the uncertainty by the value itslef gives a dimensionless quantity and gives the fractional uncertainty. Percentaging the fractional uncertainty gives the percentage uncertainty.

Propagation of uncertainties

More often than not, measurements gathered from experiments will be used in an equation to determine another quantity. The uncertainty of this calculated value will be determined by a combination of the uncertainties from the measurements used to calculate the new value. This is known as propogation of uncertainties. For the examples below, $a, b, c,$ are the quantities and $\Delta a, \Delta b, \Delta c$ are the absolute uncertainties in these quantities.

Addition and subtraction

For adding or subtracting quantities, just add together their absolute uncertainties.

If $a = b + c$ or $a = b - c$ then $ \Delta a = \Delta b + \Delta c$

Multiplication and division

When multiplying or dividing quantities then the calculated quantitiy's uncertainty is the sum of their fractional or percentage uncertainties.

If $a = bc$ or $a = {b \over c}$

then ${\Delta a \over a} = {\Delta b \over b} + {\Delta c \over c}$

1.3 Vectors and scalars

Vector and scalar quantites

Scalar quantities are quantities that only have a magnitude but no specified direction. For example speed is a scalar quantity, it is always positive and has no specified direction.

Vector quantitities are those that have both a magnitude and a direction. For example the vector equivalent for speed is velocity.

Representing vector quantities

A vector quantity is represented by a line with an arrow. The direction the arrow points in represents the direction of the vector and the length of the arrow represents the magnitude of the vector to a chosen scale. When solving calculations with vectors, it is important to assign one direction as positive and the other as negative. Which way round it is doesn't matter, as long as throughout the calculation it is constant.

Instantaneous vs average values

Instantaneous speed is the value of speed at the moment in time at which the speed is determined. In a distance-time graph the instantaneous speed is the gradient of the graph at the instant concerned. From a mathematical perspective the instantaneous speed is equal to the rate of change of position with repsect to time.($ds \over dt$). Average speed however is calculated over the whole journey without regard to variations in speed. $$\text{average speed} = {\text{distance travelled over whole journey} \over \text{time taken for whole journey}}$$ For a distance time graph the average speed is equal to the gradient of the straight line that connects the start and end point of the time interval concerned.

Motion and graphs

Figure 1 shows a distance-time graph. A distance time graph shows distance travelled from source on the y axis and time elasped on the x axis. From this graph we can determine the speed by calculating the gradient of the graph. The speed in this case: $\text{speed} = {2500 \over 1500} \approx 1.7 \text{ms}^2$. The area under the curve of a distance-time graph does not produce anything useful. A linear line indicates constant speed, a curved line indicates either acceleration or deceleration.

Figure 2 shows a velocity-time graph. The gradient of a velocity-time graph shows the acceleration at the given instance. The area underneath the curve is the distance travelled.

The "suvat" equations of motion

s - displacement

u - initial velocity/speed

a - acceleration

t - time taken to travel the distance s.

Analysis of the distance-time and speed-time graph lead to a set of equations useful in motion calculations. These equations are known as the suvat equations or the kinematic equations of motion.

Projectile motion

Considering projectile motion in two dimensions there are two components to projectile motion. There is the horizontal component and the vertical component. An object moving in projectile motion. Take for example a ball being projected from a cannon. The ball will move horizontally and vertically at the same time but the vertical and horizontal are independant of one another. Assuming that air resistance is negligible, there are no forces in the horizontal direction, so there is no horizontal acceleration. There is a constant vertical force acting downward (gravity, -9.81ms2), acceleration due to gravity.

Fluid resistance and free-fall

When an object moves through a fluid (liquid or gas) there will be frictional fluid resistance that acts against the direction of movement. This will affect the object's motion through the fluid. The drag force produced depends upon the relative velocity of the object with respect to the fluid, shape and size of the object and the viscosity of the fluid used.

Newton's laws of motion

Newton's first law

Newton's first law of motion states that "an object continues to remain stationary or to move at a constant velocity unless an external force acts on it." All this law says is that a resultant force will lead to acceleration. When the resultant force on an object then it is said to be in translational equilibrium. Mathematically: $\Sigma F = zero$

Types of forces:

Name of force Description
Gravitational force The force between objects as a result of their masses. This is sometimes reffered to the weight of the object.
Electrostatic force The force between objects as a result of their electric charges.
Magnetic force The force between magnets and/or electric currents.
Normal reaction The force between two surfaces that acts at rightangles to the surfaces. If two surfaces are smooth then this is the only force that acts between them.
Friction The force that opposes the relative motion of two surfaces and acts along the surfaces. Air resistance or drag can be thought of a frictional force - technically known as fluid friction.
Tension When a string or spring is stretched, it has equal and opposite forces on its ends pulling outwards. The tension force is the force that the end of the string applies to another object.
Compression When a rod is compressed, it has equal and opposite forces acting on its ends pushing inwards. The compression force is the force that the ends of the rods applies to another object. This is the opposite of the tension force.
Upthrust This is the upward force that acts on an object when it is submerged in a fluid. It is the buoyancy force that causes some object to float in water.
Lift This force can be exerted on an object when a fluid flows over it in an asymetrical way. The shape of the wing of an aircraft causes the aerodynamic lift that enables the aircraft to fly.

Newton's second law

Newton's second law states that: Force = mass $\times$ acceleration or $F = ma$ where the resultant force (F) is in newtons, the mass (m) is in kilograms and the acceleration (a) is in ms-2

This is the simplified version of the formula, used when there is a constant force acting on a constant mass giving a constant acceleration. If the force or mass is changing then the equation to use is: $$F = {\Delta p \over \Delta t}$$ where $p$ is momentum of a body.

Newton's third law

Newton's third law of motion states that 'when two bodies interact, the force exerted by one body will be equal and opposite in magnitude to the force exerted on that body by the other. Simply put: Every action has anequal and opposite reaction.

Free-body diagrams

In a free-body diagram only one object from a scenario is chosen and all the force acting on that object are shown and labled. Vector forces are represented as arrows and the object in question is drawn as a single point.

Static friction vs dynamic friction

Static friction

Static friction occurs when there is no relative movement between the surfaces. The static friction force $F$f ≤ $\mu$s$R$ where the symbol $\mu$s is the coefficient of static friction. The less than or equal symbol means that the static friction force can vary from zero to a maximum value. Within these limits, $F$f is equal to the pull or push on an object. When the external force acting on the object is equal to $F$max then the block is just about to move. The value of $F$max depends upon the nature of the two surfaces in contact and the normal reaction force between the two surfaces.

Dynamic friction

Dynamic friction occurs when two surfaces in contact move relative to one another. The friction between these surfaces drops from its maximum static value and becomes constant. For dynamic friction: $F$f = $\mu$d$R$ where $\mu$d is the coefficient of dynamic friction.
