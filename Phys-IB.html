Topic 1: Measurements and Uncertainties

1.1 Measurements in Physics

Quantities and units

Fundamental quantities are quantities from which all other quantities are expressed in terms of. For example in the density equation $ρ = {m \over V}$ only mass is a fundamental (volume being the product of three lengths, another fundamental unit), density and volume are said to be derived quantities.

To prevent confusion on conversions of multiple scales of units, there is a worldwide standard for units called SI - Système international d'unités. The SI defines both units and prefixes - letters used to form decimal multiples or sub-multiples of the units. The units themselves are classified as being either fundamental (or base), derived, and supplementary. There are only two supplementary units: the radian (rad) – the unit of angular measurement and the steradian (sr) – the unit of “solid angle”.

Fundamental and derived units

In SI units there are only seven fundamental units. These are:

metre (m): the length of the path travelled by light in a vacuum during a time interval of $1 \over 299 792 458$ of a second.

kilogram (kg): mass equal to the mass of the international prototype of the kilogram kept at the Bureau International des Poids et Mesures at Sèveres, near Paris.

second (s): the duration of 9192631770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the caesium-133 atom

ampere (A): that constant current which, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of in nite length, negligible circular cross-section, and placed 1 m apart in vacuum, would produce between these conductors a force equal to 2 × 10–7 newtons per metre of length.

kelvin (K): the fraction $1 \over 273.16$ of the thermodynamic temperature of the tripple point of water.

mole (mol): the amount of substance of a system that contains as many elementary entities as there are atoms in 0.012 kg of carbon–12. When the mole is used, the elementary entities must be speci ed and may be atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, other particles, or speci ed groups of such particles.

candela (cd): the luminous intensity, in a given direction, of a source that emits monochromatic radiation of frequency 540 × 1012 hertz and that has a radiant intensity in that direction of $1 \over 683$ watt per steradian.

Significant Figures

Scientists use a method of rounding to a certain number of significant figures (often abbreviated to s.f.). Here "significant" means meaningful.

Rules for using significant figures:

A digit that is not a zero will always be significant – 683 is three significant figures (3 s.f.).

Zeros that occur sandwiched between non-zero digits are always significant – 5802 (4 s.f.); 19.0405 (6 s.f.).

Non-sandwiched zeros that occur to the left of a non-zero digit are not significant – 0.389 (3 s.f); 0.093 (2 s.f.).

Zeros that occur to the right of the decimal point are significant, provided that they are to the right of a non-zero digit – 1.034 (4 s.f.); 1.00 (3 s.f.); 0.34500 (5 s.f.); 0.003 (1 s.f.).

When there is no decimal point, trailing zeros are not significant (to make them significant there needs to be a decimal point) – 400 (1 s.f.); 400. (3 s.f.) – but this is rarely written.

For addition and subtraction, leave your answers in terms of the least number of decimal places. For multiplying or dividing leave your answers to the least number of significant figures from the quantities used in the question.

Scientific notation

Scientific notation is a mathematical expression used to represent a decimal number between 1 and 10 multiplied by ten, so you can write large numbers using less digits. An example of scientific notation is when you write 6.34 x 10³ for 6340.

Metric multipliers (prefixes)

There is another way of abbreviating units: by using metric multipliers (usually called “prefixes”). An SI prefix is a name or associated symbol that is written before a unit to indicate the appropriate power of 10. So instead of writing 2.5 × 1012 J we could alternatively write this as 2.5 TJ (terajoule). These prefixes can be found in the data booklet used in IB examinations.

Orders of magnitude

It is important to understand whether or not the physics or the value calculated in physics is sensible or resonable. A number rounded to the nearest 10 is known as an order of magnitude. For example, for the average mass of a human being: around 60-80 kg - powers of 102 should be used rather than 101 as it is closer to 100 kg than 10 kg.

Estimation

Estimation is used to produce a value that is a useable approximation to the true value. Estimation is related to finding orders of magnitude, but may result in a value more precise than the nearest power of 10. For example whenever you use a ruler calibrated in millimetres you can accurately measure to a millimetre but will need to estimate to the next $1 \over 10$mm.

1.2 Uncertainties and Errors

Uncertainties in measurement

No experimental quantity can be measured with 100% accuracy, there is always a degree of uncertainty. There are two types of error that contribute to this uncertainty in scentific readings: systematic and random.

Systematic errors

These errors occur due to a fault of the system. this could be due to faulty equipment or apparatus. For example digital equipment may be incorrectly calibrated due to the way it was manufactured or due to being overused. On a piece of equipment such as a mass balance the zero setting can drift such that it no longer reads zero when it should. This is called a zero error. Systematic errors can also be due to faults in the experiment and the way it is designed. When systematic errors are small, a measurement is said to be accurate.

Random errors

Random errors can occur in any measurement but occur most frequently when the experimenter has to estimate the last significant figure when reading a scale. These are likely to cause the results of the measurements to be either too big or too small. For an analogue scale, the uncertainty is ± half of the smallest scale division. For a digital scale, the uncertainty is ± 1 the least significant digit. Repeated tests help to reduce the random errors in an experiment. Readings with small random errors are said to be precise (NOT accurate).

Absolute and fractional uncertainties

Absolute uncertainties have the same units as the the quantity itself. Dividing the uncertainty by the value itslef gives a dimensionless quantity and gives the fractional uncertainty. Percentaging the fractional uncertainty gives the percentage uncertainty.

Propagation of uncertainties

More often than not, measurements gathered from experiments will be used in an equation to determine another quantity. The uncertainty of this calculated value will be determined by a combination of the uncertainties from the measurements used to calculate the new value. This is known as propogation of uncertainties. For the examples below, $a, b, c,$ are the quantities and $\Delta a, \Delta b, \Delta c$ are the absolute uncertainties in these quantities.

Addition and subtraction

For adding or subtracting quantities, just add together their absolute uncertainties.

If $a = b + c$ or $a = b - c$ then $ \Delta a = \Delta b + \Delta c$

Multiplication and division

When multiplying or dividing quantities then the calculated quantitiy's uncertainty is the sum of their fractional or percentage uncertainties.

If $a = bc$ or $a = {b \over c}$

then ${\Delta a \over a} = {\Delta b \over b} + {\Delta c \over c}$

Instantaneous vs average values

Instantaneous speed is the value of speed at the moment in time at which the speed is determined. In a distance-time graph the instantaneous speed is the gradient of the graph at the instant concerned. Average speed however is calculated over the whole journey without regard to variations in speed. $$\text{average speed} = {\text{total distance travelled} \over \text{time taken}}$$ For a distance time graph the average speed is equal to the gradient of the straight line that connects the start and end point of the time interval concerned.

Static friction vs dynamic friction

Static friction

Static friction occurs when there is no relative movement between the surfaces. The static friction force $F$f ≤ $\mu$s$R$ where the symbol $\mu$s is the coefficient of static friction. The less than or equal symbol means that the static friction force can vary from zero to a maximum value. Within these limits, $F$f is equal to the pull or push on an object. When the external force acting on the object is equal to $F$max then the block is just about to move. The value of $F$max depends upon the nature of the two surfaces in contact and the normal reaction force between the two surfaces.

Dynamic friction

Dynamic friction occurs when two surfaces in contact move relative to one another. The friction between these surfaces drops from its maximum static value and becomes constant. For dynamic friction: $F$f = $\mu$d$R$ where $\mu$d is the coefficient of dynamic friction.

2.4 Momentum

The product of the mass $m$ of an object and its instantaneous velocity $v$ is called the momentum $p$ of the object. Momentum is mass $\times$ velocity NOT mass $\times$ speed. Momentum is a vector quantity and thus has a direction. When mass and velocity is multiplied together, the product formed, momentum, will have a direction equal to that of the velcity. Momentum has the units kg m s$^{-1}$. If the velocity or mass is changing then the momentum will also be changing. If there is a net resultant force acting on an object then it accelerates. If it accelerates then this causes a change in velocity leading to a change in momentum. Therefore a net force leads to a change in momentum.

Collisions and changing momentum

Any interaction where momentum has been transfered is called a collision. The principle of conservation of linear momentum states that momentum is always constant is no external force acts on the system. The word 'linear' is used here becuase this is momentum when the objects in question are moving in straight lines.

Two objects with the same mass, one initially stationary when no energy is lost

This is known as an elastic collision and for it to occur no permanent deformation must occur in the colliding objects and no energy can be released as internal energy (through friction), sound, or any other way.

The first object collides with the second stationary object. The first object then stays at rest whilst the second moves off with the same velocity as the first object pre collision. momentum is conserved since: $$m_1 u = m_2 v$$ Because $m_1$ = $m_2$ then $u = v$ so the velocity of one mass before the collision is equal to the velocity of the second mass afterwards. The kinetic energy of the moving mass (whichever mass is moving) is ${1 \over 2} m u^2$ and does not change either.

Two objects with different masses when no energy is lost

Again: $m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$ Here it is harder to eliminate the masses. However we do know that kinetic energy is conserved. Therfore KEbefore = KEafter as no energy is lost. This means that by summing the kinetic energies before and after the collision we get: $${1 \over 2}m_1 u_1^2 + {1 \over 2}m_2 u_2^2 = {1 \over 2}m_1 v_1^2 + {1 \over 2}m_2 v_2^2$$ These momentum and kinetic energy equations can be solved to show that: $$v_1 = ({{m_1 - m_2} \over {m_1 + m_2}})u_1 + ({{2m_2} \over {m_1 + m_2}})u_2$$ and $$v_2 = ({{m_2 - m_1} \over {m_1 + m_2}})u_2 + ({{2m_1} \over {m_1 + m_2}})u_1$$

Cases for when mass $m_2$ is stationary and collides with $m_2$:

Case 1: $m_1$ is much smaller than $m_2$. Using the $v_1$ equation, if $m_1 \ll m_2$ then the first term is roughly $({{-m_2}\over{m_2}})u_1$ which is $-u_1$. The second term is zero becuase $u_2 = 0$. So the small mass "bounces" off the large mass, reversing its direction (shown by the negative sign). The large mass gains speed in the forwards direction. The magnitude of the speed of the larger mass is roughly $({{2m_1} \over {m_2}})u_1$ and this is a small fraction of the original speed of the small mass since $({{2m_1} \over {m_2}})$ is much smaller than 1.

Case 2: $m_1$ is much greater than $m_2$. Here the original mass loses hardly any speed. The momentum lost by $m_1$ is given to $m_2$ which moves off in the same direction, but at about twice the original speed of $m_1$. Look at both $v$ equations to verify.

Two objects colliding when energy is lost

When a moving object collides with a stationary one and sticks to it, some of the initial kinetic energy is lost. After the collision there is a single object with a combined mass and single common velocity. This is known as an inelastic collision.

The momentum equation this time is $m_1 u_1 = (m_1 + m_2) v_1$ Rearranging this equation we get: $v_1 = {m_1\over m_1 + m_2}u_1$ which as we expect the final velocity is in the same direction as the initial velocity but it is always smaller.

For the energy loss, the initial kinetic energy is ${1 \over 2}m_1 u_1^2$ and the final kinetic energy is (substituting in $v_1$) is: $${1 \over 2}(m_1 + m_2){m_1^2 \over (m_1 + m_2)^2}u_1^2$$ $$={1 \over 2}{m_1^2 \over (m_1+m_2)}u_1^2$$ The ratio of $\text{initial kinetic energy} \over \text{final kinetic energy}$ is $(m_1 + m_2) \over m_1$

Two objects when energy is gained

There are cases where stationary objects gain energy in some way. To demonstrate attach small strong magnets to laboratory dynamics carts such that when they are released the repulsion drive the carts apart.

The initial momentum is zero as neither objects are in motion. After the collision the momentum: $m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = 0$ so $m_1 v_1 = –m_2 v_2$ The objects move in opposite directions. If the masses are equal the speeds are the same with the velocities having negative signs to one another. If the masses are not equal then: ${m_1 \over m_2} = {–v_2 \over v_1}$

Energy an momentum

Kinetic energy, $E_K = {1 \over 2}mu^2$; momentum, $p = mu$ and therefore $p^2 = m^2 u^2$. So: $$E_K = {p^2\over 2m}$$

Applications of momentum conservation

Recoil of a gun

When a gun is fired, the gun moves in the opposite direction (backwards) to that of the shell. This can be explained in terms of momentum conservation. To begin with, both the gun and the shell are stationary, the initial momentum is zero. When the gun is fired the shell is propelled in the forwards direction. In this case, a collision occurs where kinetic energy is gained. The explosion from the gun is internal to the system. The explosion generates a gas at high pressure which exerts a force on the shell and hence on the gun as well. The explosive releases energy which is transferred as kinetic energy to the shell and the gun. Since the inital linear momentum was zero and no external force has acted on the system the momentum must continue to be zero. This can only happen if the shell and the gun move in opposite directions. The shell will move very fast because it has a small mass compared to the gun, the gun will move relatively slowly.

Water hoses

When a garden hose is connected it can start to shoot backwards in unpredictable directions if it is not held when the tap is turned on. The cross-sectional area of the hose is greater than the nozzle from which the water emerges. The mass of water flowing through a point in the hose each second is the same as the mass flowing out of the nozzle. Therfore the speed of the water flowing out of the nozzle needs to be much greater than the speed of the water along the hose. Therfore water gains momentum as it leaves the hose due to this increased speed. The momentum of the system has to be constant so there must be a force backwards on the end of the hose. The momenum lost by the system per second is (mass of water leaving per second) $\times$ (speed at which water leaves the nozzle – speed in the hose). The mass of water lost per second is $\Delta m \over \Delta t$ and so the momentum lost per second $p$ is ${\Delta m \over \Delta t}(v-u)$ where $v$ is the speed of the water as it leaves the nozzle and $u$ is the speed of the water in the hose.

If we know the area A of the nozzle and the density $\rho$ of water then $\Delta m \over \Delta t$ can be determined. From the diagram above the cylinder is the volume of water leaving the nozzle persecond where v is the length and A is the area. The volume of water leaving per second is therefore Av. $\Delta m \over \Delta t$ = (density of water) $\times$ (volume of cylinder) = $\rho$Av.

Impusle

Newton's second law of motion can be rearranged to get: $a = {(v-u) \over t}$ Eliminating a gives: $F = {m(v–u) \over t}$
Which means that $\text{force} = {\text{change in momentum} \over \text{time taken for change}}$ or force $\times$ time = change in momentum

The product of force and time is called the impulse, with units of N s which is an alternate unit for momentum, kg m s$^{–1}$.

Force-time graphs

If a constant force acts on a mass, the graph of force F against time t will look like the first graph. The shaded area between the curve and the $x$ axis is equivalent to the change in momentum (from $F = {\Delta p \over \Delta t}$). The second graph shows force increasing at a constant rate to a maximum $F_{\text{max}}$ then falls back to zero with a total time T. Here the change in momentum is still the are under the curve calculated by: ${1 \over 2}F_{\text{max}}T$. The final graph shows a variable force against time where there seems to be no obvious relationship between the two. To calculate the area you can estimate the number of squares under the graph and use the are of one square to evaluate the change in momentum.

Revisting Newton's Second Law

Through the impulse subtopic, we know that $F = {\Delta p \over \Delta t}$. Expanding this we get: $F = {\Delta (mv) \over \Delta t}$. This can be written to show that: $F = m {\Delta v \over \Delta t} + v {\Delta m \over \Delta t}$. The first part represents $\text{mass} \times {\text{change in velocity} \over \text{change in time}}$ which can be simplified to mass $\times$ acceleration. The second part represents the instantaneous velocity $\times {\text{change in mass} \over \text{change in time}}$. The extra term takes in account what happens when the mass of an accelerating object is changing.

Momentum and safety

When a car is involved in a collision, the occupant in the car has to lose the same amount of kinetic energy and momentum. This can be via smashing into the windscreen or by being restrained by a seatbelt. With the former case, the time taken for the change in momentum is small, therefore the force will be very large (f $\times$ t = momentum change). The higher the force, the more damage. Seat belts and airbags increase the time taken for occupants in the car to slow down and stop. Thus for a constant change in momentum, a longer stopping time should lead to a smaller and less damaging force.

Topic 3: Thermal Physics

3.1 Temperature and energy changes

Temperature and energy transfer

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules in a substance. The relative temperature of two objects determines the direction in which energy passes from one object to another; energy will tend to flow from the hotter object to the colder object until both are at the same temperature. When this occurs the objects are in thermal equilibrium. The energy flowing as a result of convection, conduction or thermal radiation is called "heat".

Temperature is a scalar quantity and is measured in units of degrees Celsius ($^\circ$C) or kelvin (K) using a thermometer.

Absolute temperature

The Celsius temperature scale is based off the tripple point of water. The absolute temperature scale is the standard SI temperature scale which uses the kelvin for units. Absolute temperature is defined to be zero kelvin at absolute zero (the temperature at which all objects have a minimum energy) and 273.16 K at the tripple point of water. An increase of 1 $^\circ$C is an increase of 1 K. Therefore, converting Celsius to absolute temperatures: T(K) = $\theta (^\circ C) + 273$ where T is the absolute temperature and $\theta$ the temperature in Celsius.

Internal energy

Substances consist of particles in constant motion (except at absolute zero). When energy is transferred to a substance the particles move faster and spread out further away from each other. As their separation increases, their potential also increases. As they move faster the amount of kinetic energy they have increases. The internal energy of a substance is the total of the potential and kinetic energies of all the particles in the substance.

Specific heat capacity

The specific heat capacity (c) of a substance is the energy required to increase the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K. The equation for this is:$$c = {Q \over m \Delta T}$$ where $Q$ is the amount of energy supplied to an object of mass $m$ causing its temperature to rise by $\Delta T$. When $Q$ is in J, $m$ in kg, and $\Delta T$ in K, c will have the units of J kg$^{-1}$K$^{-1}$.

Specific latent heat

When an substance undergoes a phase change, its temperature does not increase. This is because the energy is going to overcoming the intermolecular forces between the molecules.

Specific latent heat of fusion is the energy required to change the phase of 1 kg of a substance from a solid to a liquid without any change in temperature.

Specific latent heat of vaporization is the energy required to change the phase of 1 kg of a substance from a liquid to a gas without any change in temperature.

The equations for specific latent heat take the form: $L = {Q \over m}$.

Molecular explanation of phase change

Transferring energy to a solid increases it's internal energy. Eventually some of the molecules in the solid move far enough away from one another to lose the influence of the other molecules (intermolecular bonds being broken). When this happens, the kinetic energy of the molecules does not increase, instead the energy is used to increase the potential energy of the molecules. Eventually these groups of molecules are sufficiently free so that the solid has melted. The average kinetic energy of the molecule begins to rise again, increasing the temperature of the liquid. The molecules are breaking away from each other and reforming at an equal rate, thus there is no overall change in potential energy. When the liquid reaches its boiling point, individual molecules break away from the entire liauid and the potential begins to rise once more. Here the mean kinetic energy remains constant until all the molecules have separated from each other.

Kinetic theory

Macroscopic/Microscopic

The macroscopic point of view considers the system as a whole and sees how it interacts with its surroundings.
The microscopic point of view looks inside the system to see how its component parts interact with one another.

Solids - Macroscopically, solids have a fixed volume and shape. This is becuase the molecules are held in place by strong intermolecular bonds. The molecules are able to vibrate slightly around an average position. The higher the temperature, the larger the vibrations.
Liquids - Liquids also have a fixed volume but its shape is defined by the container it's in. These molecules also vibrate but are free to roll around each other.
Gases - A gas will always expand to fill the container its in. The molecules are not fixed in place and the intermolecular forces between molecules is very weak.

3.2 Modelling a gas

The gas laws

An ideal gas follows gas laws under all conditions. Real gases do not do this but they approximate to a real gas as long as pressure is slightly higher than atmospheric pressure. Boyle's law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume. Mathematically: $p \propto {1 \over V}$ (at constant temperature) or $pV = \text{constant}$ (at constant temperature). A graph of this (pressure against volume at constant temperature) is known as an isothermal curve. "Iso" meaning the same and "thermal" meaning temperature.

Charles' law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure the volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. Mathematically: $V \propto T$ (at constant pressure) or ${V \over T} = \text{constant}$ (at constant pressure).

The third gas law (pressure law) states for a gas of fixed mass and volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. Mathematically: $p \propto T$ (at constant volume) or ${p \over T} = \text{constant}$ (at constant volume).

Italian physicist Count Amadeo Avagadro found that: the number of particles in a gas at constant temperature and pressure is directly proportional to the volume of the gas. Mathematically: $n \propto V$ (at constant pressure and temperature) or ${n \over V} = \text{constant}$ (at constant pressure and temperature).

These four gas laws can be combined to give and ideal gas constant $R$ which has the value of apporximately 8.314 J mol$^{-1}$ K${^-1}$. Combining the four gas laws gives the equation of state of an ideal gas: $${pV \over nT} = R \text{ or } pV = nRT$$ Where pressure is measured in pascal (Pa), volume in cubic metre (m$^3$), temperature in kelvin (K), and n is the number of moles in the gas.

The mole and the Avagadro constant

The mole, mol, is a measure of how much of a substance something has. It is one of the SI base units defined as: the amount of substance having the same number of particles as there are neutron atoms in 12 grams of carbon-12. One mole of a substance will have 6.02 $\times$ 10$^{23}$ atoms or molecules. This is known as the Avagadro constant NA

The microscopic interpretation of gases

Diffusion

Diffusion is a process resulting from the random motion of particles which causes a net flow of particles from regions of high concentration to regions of lower concentration.

Brownian motion

Brownian motion is the irregular (random) motion of suspended particles in fluids due to the bombardment of the particles by the molecules of the medium.

Kinetic model of an ideal gas

Assumptions:
↦ Newtons's law applies to molecular behaviour
↦ There are no intermolecular forces except during a collision
↦ The molecules are treated as points
↦ The molecules are in random motion
↦ The collisions between the molecules and the walls of their containing vessel are perfectly elastic (no energy is lost)
↦ There is no time spent in these collisions

Using the assumptions above it is possible to derive the ideal gas equation:

Imagine a box like the one above with volume $V$ that has $N$ molecules moving in it each with mass $m$. The box is a cube with side lengths $L$. Consider the collision of one molecule moving with velocity $c$ towards the right hand wall of the box. The components of the velocity in the x, y, z directions are $c_x, c_y, c_z$ respectively. As the molecule collides with the wall with perfect elasticity, the x component of velocity is reversed whilst its y and z components remain unchanged.
The x component of the momentum of the molecule is $mc_x$ before the collision and $-mc_x$ after the collision. The change in momentum is therefore: $-mc_x-(mc_x) = -2mc_x$. The force $F_x$ exerted on the molecule by the wall is therefore $-2mc_x \over t$ where t is the time taken by the molecule to travel from the right-hand wall of the box to the opposite side and back (the time between collisions with the right-hand wall of the box).
Thus $t = {2L \over c_x}$ and so $F_m = {-2mc_x \over {2L \over c_x}}$ $ = {-mc_x^2 \over L}$
Using Newton's third law, it is clear that the molecule must exert a force of $mc_x^2 \over L$ on the right-hand wall of the box. However there are N molecules in the box that have a range of speeds so the total force exerted on the right hand wall of the box is: $$F_x = {m \over L} (c^2_{x_1} + c^2_{x_2} + c^2_{x_3} + ... + c^2_{x_N})$$ The mean value of the square of the velocities is given by: $$\bar{c^2_x} = {(c^2_{x_1} + c^2_{x_2} + c^2_{x_3} + ... +c^2_{x_N}) \over N}$$Giving the total force on the right-hand wall of the box to be: $F_x = {Nm \over L} \bar{c^2_x}$. Using Pythagoras' theorem we can see that a 3-d velocity vector with components $c_x, c_y, c_z$ can be used to resolve the vector using the equation: $$\bar{c^2} = \bar{c_x^2} + \bar{c_y^2} + \bar{c_z^2}$$ where $\bar{c^2}$ is the mean square speed of the speed of the molecules. On average the probability of a molecule moving in one direction is equal to it moving in any other direction so the magnitude of the mean components of the velocity will be equal. $$\bar{c^2_x} = \bar{c^2_y} = \bar{c^2_z} \text{ so } \bar{c^2_x} = {1 \over 3}\bar{c^2}$$ So the equation for the total force on the right hand wall becomes $F = {1 \over 3} {Nm \over L^3} \bar{c^2}$
The pressure on this wall is given by $p_x = {F_x \over L^2}$ so that $p_x = {1 \over 3} {Nm \over L^3} \bar{c^2}$ or $p_x = {1 \over 3} {Nm \over V} \bar{c^2}$. Since the pressure in a fluid acts equally in all directions the equation can be written as: $p = {1 \over 3} {Nm \over V} \bar{c^2}$
N being the number of molecules and m being the mass of the molecules, $Nm \over V^3$ is the density, $\rho$, simplifying the equation to: $p = {1 \over 3} \rho \bar{c^2}$

This proof doen not need to be learnt line by line but each step should make sense to you.

Molecular interpretation of temperature

Rearranging $p = {1 \over 3} {Nm \over V} \bar{c^2}$ we get $pV = {Nm \over 3} \bar{c^2}$
Multiplying throughout by $3 \over 2$: ${3 \over 2} pV = N \times {1 \over 2}m \bar{c^2}$. Comparing this with the ideal gas equation pV = nRT we can see that ${3 \over 2}nRT = N \times {1 \over 2} m \bar{c^2}$.

4.1 Oscillations

Isochronous oscillations

Isochronus oscillations are oscillations that repeat in the same time period no matter what the amplitude is. An example of this is a pendulum. A stroboscope (strobe) can be used to freeze an isochronous oscillation such that it appears to be stationary. This is done by making the strobe flash at regular intervals that match the oscillations.

Describing periodic motion

The time period or simply period(T) of the motion is the time duration for one full cylce.

Here the time for 10 oscillations is 12 seconds giving a period of 1.2 seconds. As the pendulum passes its rest or equilibrium position its displacment (x) is zero. As displacement is a vector quantity it has a direction specified on the graph as a positive or negative displacement. The graph is positive to begin with so the pendulum is moving to the right (right is taken to be positive).

The maximum value of the displacement is called the amplitude ($x_0$). The frequency (f) of an oscillations is the number of oscillations completed per unit time. When time is in seconds the units for frequency is hertz (Hz). The relationship between the frequency and time period is: $$T = {1 \over f}$$

Simple harmonic motion

In order for an object to be undergoing SHM it must have a restoring force acting on it:
↦ The magnitude of the force (and therefore acceleration) is proportional to the displacement of the body from a fixed point.
↦ The direction of the force is always towards that fixed point.
The fixed point is the equilibrium position of the mass. In a pendulum, at equilibrium gravity is acting on soley in the y axis, equal and opposite to the tension in the string holding the mass. To the right of equilibrium, a component of tension acts in the x axis, to the left pulling it back to the equilibrium point. To the left of equilibrium, a component of tension acts to the rihtg in the x axis. The difference between the tension and the weight provides the restoring force - one that tends to return the mass to its equilibrium point. This relationship between acceleration $a$ and displacement $x$ can be expressed as: $$a \propto - x$$ which is equal to $$a = - kx$$

Phase and phase difference

Phase ↦ Position of an oscillating body in its cycle.
Phase difference ↦ Difference in phase between two oscillations.
The period T is equivalent to 360$^{\circ}$ or 2$\pi$ radians. Therefore $T \over 2$ = $180^{\circ}$ or $\pi$ radians.

Energy changes in SHM

Imagine a pendulum undergoing SHM like the one in the diagram above. For a pendulum to undergo SHM, the length of the string needs to be long and the angle of the swings need to be small, less than 10$^\circ$. When the pendulum is pulled to position A, it is at its highest point and has a maximum gravitational potential energy, minimum kinetic energy. As the pendulum passes through the equlibrium position B, all of the GPE is converted to KE, here it has a maximum speed. Moving back up to position C, the pendulum loses all kinetic energy and regains GPE.

4.2 Travelling Waves

There are two fundamental types of waves:
↦ mechanical waves - requires material medium through which to travel i.e. sound
↦ electromagnetic waves - can travel through a vacuum i.e. light

Travelling waves

Travelling waves are waves that have a net energy flow. A wave is generated from a source from where it travels away from. There are two types of travelling waves: Longitudinal and Transverse. With a transverse wave, the direction the wave travels in is perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer. With a longitudunal wave the direction of motion of the wave is parallel to the direction of energy transfer.

Describing waves

↦ Wavelength $\lambda$ is the shortest distance between two points that are in phase on a wave. i.e. two consecutive maximums. It is measured in meters (m).
↦ Frequency $f$ is the number complete cycles of an oscillating particle per unit time. It is measures in Hertz (hz).
↦ Period $T$ is the time that it takes for one complete wavelength to pass through a fixed point, i.e. the time taken for one oscillation. It is usually measured in milliseconds (ms).
↦ Amplitude $A$ is the maximum displacement of the particle from its equilibrium position. It is measured in meters (m).
