Topic 1: Stoichiometric Relationships

1.1 Introduction to the particulate nature of matter and chemical change

The idea that matter is made up of discrete units has been around for thousands of years. These ideas were founded using philosophical and theological reasoning rather than evidence and experiment based research. Due to this atomism was only considered a competing theory among others on the nature of matter. It was not until the nineteenth century that the idea was fully embraced and refined by the scientific community. By the start of the nineteenth century there were three main important theories:

1) The law of conservation of mass states that matter cannot be created or desroyed, it is always conserved.

2) The law of definite proportions states that 'no matter how you make a chemical compound, its got the same ratio of elements. An example: Whether you make water by combining hydrogen and oxygen or by decomposing hydrogen peroxide, the resulting water will still be 1 part by mass hydrogen to 8 parts by mass oxygen.'

3) 'The law of multiple proportions refers to different chemical compounds that can be formed when two elements react with each other. For example, hydrogen and oxygen can react to form water (H2O) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). In the first compound, one gram of hydrogen reacts with eight grams of oxygen. In the second, one gram of hydrogen reacts with sixteen grams of oxygen. If we look at the ratio of these to each other, 16/8 = 2, which is a whole number. The law of multiple proportions says that whenever oxygen and hydrogen (or any two elements react to form more than one compound), the ratios of the elements to each other will be some whole number multiple of the ratios of the elements in the other compounds.'

Elements, Compounds & Mixtures

Allotrope: Different structural form of the same element. Eg: Diamond, graphite and graphene are all allotropes of carbon.

Diatomic: Molecule containing two atoms. Eg: Oxygen - O2

Compound: Two or more different atoms chemically bonded. Eg: Methane - CH4

Mixture: Mixtures contain more than one element/and or compound that are not chemically bonded together and so retain their individual properties. Eg: Mixing together sugar and salt.

Comparison of mixtures and compounds:

Mixture Compound
Composition Variable composition – you can vary the amount of each substance in a mixture. Definite composition – you cannot vary the amount of each substance in a compound.
Joined or not The different substances are not chemically bonded together. The different elements are chemically bonded together.
Properties Each substance in the mixture keeps its own properties. The compound has properties different from the elements it contains.
Separation Separation is usually possible by physical means. Separation is only possible using chemical reactions.
Examples Air, seawater, most rocks. Water, carbon dioxide, silicon dioxide, magnesium chloride.

Homogenus vs Heterogenus mixtures:

Type of mixture Homogenus Heterogenus
Composition Same everywhere (i.e. evenly distributed and mixed) Not the same everywhere
Can components be distiguished by eye? No Yes
Examples Fully diffused mixture of bromine and oxygen, air Oil and water (both liquids, different phases), solid copper in water, granite

1.2 The mole concept

Relative masses

The vast majority of chemists use a relative scale to compare the different masses of atoms. All atoms are compared to the isotope Carbon-12 (chosen due to its abundance, stability and solid state). C-12 is assigned the value of 12.000. When the term relative is used it means on a scale which carbon-12 atom has a mass of exactly 12 units or relative to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom. All relative masses have no units. There are four different types of relative mass:

Type of mass Definition
Isotopic The mass of a particular isotope (relative to/on a scale of.....)
Molecular (Mr) The sum of the relative atomic masses of the atoms in one molecule (relative to/on a scale of.....)
Formula The sum of the relative atomic masses of the atoms given in the formula of an ionic compound (relative to/on a scale of.....)
Atomic (Ar) The weighted average (according to relative abundances) of all the masses of isotopes (relative to/on a scale of.....)

Numbers of particles

The relative mass of particles and the number of particles can be easily connected.

32 is the relative atomic mass of sulphur.

32g is also the mass of 6.022 x 1023 atoms of S (i.e. one moles worth of atoms)

6.022 x 1023 is known as the Avagadro's constant.

The Avagadro's constant (represented by the letter L) is defined as the number of atoms in exactly 12.000g of carbon 12.

The molar mass is the mass of a substance that contains one mole of that substance. It has the units g/mol

This leads to the well known relationship: Moles(mol) = Mass(g) / Molar Mass(g/mol)

Empirical and Molecular Formulae

Each substance has an empirical formula. It shows the simplest ratio of atoms of each element in a substance. e.g. SiO2 (giant covalent) - the ratio of Si:O atoms in the lattice is 1:2

Substances made of molecules also have a molecular formula. This indicates the actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule.

Calculation Methods:

i) Finding the empirical formula of a compound from its composition by percentage or mass

i) Write out the mass or percentage of each element

ii) Divide each mass or percentage by the Ar of the element (not the Mr)

iii) Find the simplest whole number ratio of these numbers by dividing by the smallest number. If the values come out as near 1/2's then times them by two, if they are near 1/3's then times by three.

Limiting Reagents

In the real world of chemistry, it is rare that we react the exact right amount of chemicals together. Usually, we have more than we need of a particular reactant so it doesn't all react - it is in excess. Sometimes in calculations we need to work out which one of the reactants is in excess. The reactant that is not in excess is referred to as the limiting reagent. For example:

2SO2 + O2 → 2SO3

The Gas Laws

Boyle's Law:

At constant temperature, the pressure of a gas decreases as the volume of a gas increases.
PV = Constant, P1V1 = P2V2

Charles's Law:

At constant pressure the volume of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to the temperature.
V = T x Constant, V1 / T1 = V2 / T2

The Pressure Law

At constant volume, the absolute temperature (i.e. measured in kelvin) is propotional to the pressure.
P = T x Constant, P1 / T1 = P2 / T2

Avagadro's Law

Under the same condition of temperature and pressure, equal volumes of all gases contain the same number of molecules. Eg. If 1 mole of oxygen takes up 24dm3, as will one mole of hydrogen or carbond dioxide, or any other gas - (this is true for gases under standard conditions).

The Ideal Gas Equation

The 'empirical' laws mentioned above can be combined to form:

PV = nRT

Where P is pressure in Pa, V is volume in m3, n is the number of moles in mol, R is the molar gas constant (8.31JK-1mol-1) and T is temperature in Kelvin

Ideal and Real Gases

The kinetic theory of gases

This is a model used to describe the behaviour of particles in a gas. This model can be used to work out theoretical equations to describe the behaviour of gases (ie. the Ideal Gas Law). Essentially a gas consists of molecules that are in constant random motion, in all directions throughout the container. There are many assumptions made:

Assumption Good Assumption? Comments
Particles are in a continuous state of random motion Yes -
Gas particles have negligble volume Yes This is not a good assumption at high pressures as at high pressures the volume of gases is very low which makes the volume of each individual particle significant
Gas particle collisions are perfectly elastic No Energy is being transferred but total energy is constant so not a big issue
Gas particles exert no attractive forces on each other Depends on gas Some gases have higher intermolecular forces. This is a problem at high pressures and low temperatures.
Gas particles have an average kinetic energy that is proportional to absolute temperatures. Yes Kinetic energy is proportional to temperature

How do real gases behave as we change the pressure?

At low pressures (1 atm) gas behaviour is ideal, it follows PV = nRT. At moderately high temperatures (50 - 400atm) the volume of gases is lower than expected. This deviation is due to intermolecular forces. At very high pressures (500 - 1000s atm) the volume occupied by gases is higher than expected due to repulsion and the volume of particles being no longer negligible.

How do real gases behave as we change the temperature?

The higher the temperature, the closer gases get to behaving like an ideal gas. At low temperatures and pressures the volume is lower than expected due to attractive molecule forces.

Titrations and Standard Solutions

Standard Solution

In analytical chemistry, a standard solution is a solution containing a precisely known concentration of an element or a substance. It is prepared using a standard substance, such as a primary standard. Standard solutions are used to determine the concentrations of other substances, such as solutions in titrations. Primary standards are typically a reagent that can be weighed easily, and which is so pure that its weight is truly reprasentative of the number of the number of moles of substance contained.

Features of a standard solution: Non-toxic, Stable, Highly Soluble, Reacts Quickly, High Mr

Technique for making up a standard solution:

1. Weigh any solid by difference, i.e. the sample tube must be weighed after the solid has been poured out of it.

2. Ensure the pan on the balance is clean before use and that no material is spilled on the pan during weighing.

3. Fully disolve the solid in as much water as is needed in a beaker - without exceeding the volume of the volumetric flask

4. Transfer the solution into the volumetric flask via a funnel.

5. Rinse out the beaker twice with distilled water and add to the volumetric flask.

6. Rinse the funnel. Rinse the stirring rod if used.

7. Make up to the mark by adding the final distilled water with pipette. N.B. The bottom of the meniscus

8. Mix thoroughly by inverting and shaking vigorously several times.

Types of Titrations:

Finding concentration of a solution A standard solution is made and used to find the concentration of the other solution in the titration
Finding the formula mass (mr) of a compound A standard solution is made of the compound. It is titrated with the other solution and its concentration, mol dm-3, formed. Using: moles = g/mr the value of mr is found.
Finding the percentage purity of a sample of impure compounds A standard solution of the impure solid is made. Titration with the other solution enables you to find the mass of the pure solid and hence the percentage purity
Finding the formula of a compound Exactly the same thing as finding the mr, above, but the mr value is used to find, for example, 'the x in Na2CO3.xH2O'
Finding the percentage mass of an element in a compound The method is the same as for above, but you find the mass of the element and hence the percentage of it in the compound.
Using the 'back titration' method Usually a solid is reacted with an excess of acid. The acid left over in the reaction is found by titrating with a base. It is therefore possible to find how much acid reacted with the solid and so more about the solid itself.

Titrations often involve acid base reactions or redox reactions. Eg:

Acid-base

Standardisation of sodium hydroxide using a standard solution of hydrochloric acid.

Standardisation of hydrochloric acid using a standard solution of sodium carbonate.

Estimation of the water of crystallisation in washing soda using weighed aliquots.

Determination of the molecular mass of an organic acid using standard sodium hydroxide.

Analysis of a carbonate/hydrogencarbonate mixture by the "double indicator" method.

Redox

Standardisation of solution of potassium manganate(VII) using potassium ethanedioate.

Calculation of the percentage of iron in an iron(II) compound.

Standardisation of sodium thiosulphate solution using potassium manganate(VII).

Calculation of the percentage of copper in a compound using sodium thiosulphate.

Determination of the available chlorine in bleach.

Estimation of the concentration of alcohol in blood using potassium dichromate(VI).

Complexiometric

Use of EDTA to determine the hardness of water.

Titration

Two basic techniques can be used: The solution is added to
a) a known volume solution (measured by pipette) in the flask OR
b) a known mass of solute weighed into the flask and disolved in distilled water - this method is known as titrating against weighed aliquots.

Correct Titration Technique:

1. Rinse burette with distilled water and then the solution can be used.

2. Fill the burette using a funnel; make sure the jet space is filled by running some burette solution into a beaker (but there should be no drops coming from it)

3. Remove plastic funnel from the top of the burette.

4. Read the initial volume of solution in burette to the nearest 0.05cm3 - note in results table.

5. Rinse pipette with the solution to be used.

6. Transfer solution into a (clean - rinsed with distilled water) conical flask with a pipette (remove any excess drops). You will need to use a pipette filler.

7. Allow pipette to empty under gravity (do not squirt it out).

8. When empty touch the tip of the pipette onto the surface of the solution.

9. Titrate solution from burette fairly quickly into the conical flask - swirl continuously - note final volume to the nearest 0.05cm3 - This is the ROUGH run.

10. Repeat the titration but this time add the solution from the burette quickly until the volume is 2-3 cm3 of the end point. Add the remaining solution one drop at a time, swirling after each addition.

11. When the end-point has almost been reached rinse down the tip of the burette and the inside of the conical flask to ensure all the reagents arein the solution, then continue adding a single drop at a time from the burette.

12. Repeat the titration until you achieve concordant results, i.e, two runs agree to within 0.10 cm3.
