Topic 1: Stoichiometric Relationships

1.1 Introduction to the particulate nature of matter and chemical change

The idea that matter is made up of discrete units has been around for thousands of years. These ideas were founded using philosophical and theological reasoning rather than evidence and experiment based research. Due to this atomism was only considered a competing theory among others on the nature of matter. It was not until the nineteenth century that the idea was fully embraced and refined by the scientific community. By the start of the nineteenth century there were three main important theories:

  • 1) The law of conservation of mass - Antoine Lavoisier
  • 2) The law of definite proportions - Joseph Louis Proust
  • 3) The law of multiple proportions - John Dalton

1) The law of conservation of mass states that matter cannot be created or desroyed, it is always conserved.

2) The law of definite proportions states that 'no matter how you make a chemical compound, its got the same ratio of elements. An example: Whether you make water by combining hydrogen and oxygen or by decomposing hydrogen peroxide, the resulting water will still be 1 part by mass hydrogen to 8 parts by mass oxygen.'

3) 'The law of multiple proportions refers to different chemical compounds that can be formed when two elements react with each other. For example, hydrogen and oxygen can react to form water (H2O) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). In the first compound, one gram of hydrogen reacts with eight grams of oxygen. In the second, one gram of hydrogen reacts with sixteen grams of oxygen. If we look at the ratio of these to each other, 16/8 = 2, which is a whole number. The law of multiple proportions says that whenever oxygen and hydrogen (or any two elements react to form more than one compound), the ratios of the elements to each other will be some whole number multiple of the ratios of the elements in the other compounds.'

Elements, Compounds & Mixtures

Allotrope: Different structural form of the same element. Eg: Diamond, graphite and graphene are all allotropes of carbon.

Diatomic: Molecule containing two atoms. Eg: Oxygen - O2

Compound: Two or more different atoms chemically bonded. Eg: Methane - CH4

Mixture: Mixtures contain more than one element/and or compound that are not chemically bonded together and so retain their individual properties. Eg: Mixing together sugar and salt.

Comparison of mixtures and compounds:

Mixture Compound
Composition Variable composition – you can vary the amount of each substance in a mixture. Definite composition – you cannot vary the amount of each substance in a compound.
Joined or not The different substances are not chemically bonded together. The different elements are chemically bonded together.
Properties Each substance in the mixture keeps its own properties. The compound has properties different from the elements it contains.
Separation Separation is usually possible by physical means. Separation is only possible using chemical reactions.
Examples Air, seawater, most rocks. Water, carbon dioxide, silicon dioxide, magnesium chloride.

Homogenus vs Heterogenus mixtures:

Type of mixture Homogenus Heterogenus
Composition Same everywhere (i.e. evenly distributed and mixed) Not the same everywhere
Can components be distiguished by eye? No Yes
Examples Fully diffused mixture of bromine and oxygen, air Oil and water (both liquids, different phases), solid copper in water, granite
